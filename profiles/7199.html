My thoughts about the process:

1) I originally applied to 15 schools in early June and was interviewed at 7 of them. Worried about not having enough interviews, I applied to 10 more schools in October. DO NOT DO THIS!! Of the 10 schools I applied to late in the cycle, I was interviewed at only 1. It really is important to apply early!

2) I interviewed at 8 schools total and was accepted to 5 of them (BU, Dartmouth, Pittsburgh, Yale and Harvard) and was waitlisted at 3 (UPenn, Brown, and Duke). Good interview skills raise your chances of not being rejected from the school post-interview. Practice with your friends, your relatives, and if you can apply for jobs and practice interview skills in the real world. I did all of this and felt very well prepared.

3) When deciding where to apply, go only for schools you actually see yourself attending. Of the schools that did not invite me to an interview, only Stanford and Hopkins I could imagine attending (I knew for example I could never live in a red state, but I still applied to Vandy, etc). Your motivation for doing secondaries increases if it?s a school you like.

4) When deciding how many schools to apply to follow roughly this statistic: you will be interviewed at ~50% of the ones you applied to, and you will receive acceptances from ~50% of your interviews.

5) This last piece of advice might seem trivial, but I would not be saying it if I did not see many instances of students failing to reflect on the interview dress code? realize that you are presenting yourself to your interviewers as a potential colleague! Look professional!

Specifically, FOLLOW THESE GUIDELINES (if you are female):

- avoid fingernail polish but trim your nails
- NO open-toe shoes
- avoid jewelry
- avoid makeup
- avoid perfume
- avoid fancy bags!! Your Coach bag might be nice for a day in the city with your girlfriends, but this is not the right occasion to showcase your bag collection. Go for dark, professional satchels, folders, or no bag at all.
- pant suits look more professional than skirts or dresses, and will give you more freedom to move around during your interview day
- avoid stilettos (you do not want to be clacking behind your interviewer on a polished hospital floor). Look for sturdy, soft heels that make little sound and look professional.
