Summary of Application Experience

I feel like I did so many things wrong in this process, yet the end result was better than I could have hoped for in my wildest dreams. I wouldn't recommend any of the following things, but just to give my fellow self-doubters out there some comfort...

It is still possible to get into a great medical school even if you:
-got a C+ in freshman bio (I thought this was the end of the world)
-didn't start submitting your secondaries until the end of September (SDN made me feel like a failure)
-got an O in the writing section of the MCAT (I was pretty sure this classified me as illiterate)
-had a typo on your AMCAS activities section (can't tell you how long I agonized over this)
-never volunteered in a hospital or a free clinic or the like (after a point, I thought it would appear contrived)
-didn't really know the BCPM professors that you had to request letters from (I was never one for office hours)

Again, I DO NOT recommend doing any of these things, but if they have already happened, I'd say there's no point in agonizing over it. It will probably be okay...but you might have to endure some waitlists along the way while the people with higher stats and perfect applications get their acceptances in March.

___________________________________________________

Tips about individual schools:

WAKE FOREST: I had heard from several people that sometimes you have to call them for an interview, and it was true in my case as well. After sending in my secondary, I had been 'Complete' for 2 weeks when I called the admissions office. I simply said, 'I'd like to check on the status of my application,' and the lady immediately said, 'Oh we can go ahead and schedule an interview with you.' I don't know why they do this...
Oh, and I sent a letter of interest about 2 days after being waitlisted, but nothing else before I got accepted.
_____

UNIVERSITY OF VERMONT: After I had been in the 'small pool' for a couple of weeks, I sent them a note via the application website saying that I would be in the area on a certain date and was thinking about my travel plans etc. I got an interview invite a few days later, for the date I requested. Coincidence? Maybe, maybe not.
_____

COLUMBIA: I have to say that this acceptance was about 90% miracle and 10% persistence, but I think the persistence was definitely necessary. First of all, I got one of the last interview invites, for one of the last interview days. Then, I had one 25-minute interview (on Friday the 13th, no less) that I thought went pretty badly for me. I was expecting a rejection in the mail and was actually kind of thrilled to get waitlisted...so I took that opportunity and ran with it.

4/6: Mailed a letter of intent addressed to Dr. Frantz. I told him what an honor it was to interview and that, while I was disappointed not to be accepted in March, I am very grateful to still be considered. I said that Columbia is and has been my first choice, and that being accepted would be a dream come true. I went on to talk about elements of the school's philosophy/curriculum/program that drew me to apply, and I assured him that I would take advantage of those resources. Then I talked about what I saw when I was on campus, what I thought made Columbia a *unique* institution, and I mentioned how I would contribute to that environment. In closing, I gave the 'intent' statement, saying that if admitted I would absolutely attend, and I asked for his support in my request. All in all, the letter was a page long (with a little font down-sizing).

4/20: I called the Admissions Office, told them I was on the alternate list and asked to speak with Dr. Frantz. When he came on the phone, I told him that I was calling to follow up on the letter I sent him, and then he pretty much led the conversation from there (thankfully). He gave his spiel about people not dropping their acceptances until closer to May 15 and him keeping me very much in mind. Overall, very pleasant.

5/5: Mailed another letter of intent. In order to make it different than the last, this time I emphasized why I wanted to be in NYC. I think this was pretty important since I am from the South and have never lived in a big city. I also was more specific about the contribution I saw myself making at P&S and gave examples of how I contributed to my college. In closing, I once again made the statement of intent.

5/14: I was debating about whether or not I should call again when I received the phone call of my dreams. He said that he could make a definite offer of admission if P&S was still my first choice. When I said absolutely (sounding like a bumbling fool, I'm sure), he wanted to confirm that I would also be withdrawing from any other waitlists I may be on.

Moral of the story: I am definitely not a very aggressive person when it comes to asking people for favors, calling, writing, nagging...but I'm so glad I set my fears aside and went after this one. As far as I can tell, this is what Columbia wants. It seems to me that the majority of waitlist offers go to people who made a commitment and pursued it steadfastly. Check out the waitlist threads on SDN for ways that other people got admitted. A lot of people had a second interview with Dr. Frantz, and some people even requested one, but I never had one or asked for one. Anyway, my point is that waitlists really aren't the end of the world, especially at Columbia. Since they do reject people post-interview, and other waitlisters will withdraw because they got into Harvard or the like, your chances are probably pretty good if you're willing to put in the effort.
