Brief Profile:
This page is solely meant to help future applicants.

I found that looking at mdapplicant profile pages who were accepted to medical school helped guide me somewhat. I hope future students use my profile like I used others'. If you feel like this helped you, maybe you should do the same for future applicants. As time moves on, this page will become more and more outdated since the AMCAS application constantly changes. Help future applicants by keeping your mdapps detailed.

Now that the cycle is over for me, I have added my personal statement and activities. They are copy and pasted from my AMCAS application, any grammatical errors and typos found were left there. The only thing removed are names. At the bottom of the page, you will find three update letters I sent to UWSOM that spanned five-ish months.

In addition, I applied to so many schools because I didn't receive my MCAT score (32R) until July 24th and applied before that date. I didn't know if my score would improve past the 28Q and I only wanted to apply once. Hence the absurdly long (IMO) list of schools.

Some people have asked me why I didn't attend all the interviews and only went to three out of the eight I received. Frankly put, I didn't have the money to pay to live, attend interviews, save up for moving and pay for a wedding. I attended the first three interviews I received and had Creighton accept me on the first day I could be accepted. At that point, I did not schedule additional interviews because I knew I would be happy wherever I ended up (Creighton, or if I was accepted to UW or VCU) and was going to be a doctor. In January I was told I received a scholarship to Creighton that would make tuition cheaper than the other medical schools and that further encouraged me to not attend the other interviews.

Research Activities

Undergraduate Lab Technician

Clinical Activities

*Hospital Volunteering*
Aug 2009 to May 2011
I volunteered at the Pullman Regional Hospital from August 2009 to May 2011. I volunteered in the ER for 108 hours and the birthplace for 24 hours, for a total of 132 hours. My main job in the ER started out as just changing linen after the patient left but I soon began to record vital signs, restock rooms, help clean wounds, and to observe the doctors and nurses when the opportunity arose. Other duties included checking on patients and talking to them during the course of their stay. Towards the end of my experience, doctors allowed me to follow them, look at charts and see x-rays, as well as ask questions about treatment options.

Most Meaningful

At Pullman Regional Hospital, I originally volunteered in the birthplace. I would check on patients, ask if I could bring them anything and restock the supplies in the rooms. After doing this for a few months, I chose to explore different areas of the hospital. On my first day in the ER, however, I decided that I would spend a majority of my volunteer hours there because I could interact with patients much more frequently. In addition, the doctors and nurses in the ER were extremely helpful and insightful. In my free time, I could ask them about their professions and daily experiences and I found myself more attracted to medicine. I liked the quick pace and knowledge level required to be an efficient doctor, and I believed that I had the skills to be that. I loved talking with doctors, looking at x-rays, urine and blood tests and then applying my knowledge from classes to help diagnose the patients. I found that I interacted well with patients, giving them confidence and alleviating worries. Patients would often tell me that I helped them calm down, which is essential in the interaction between doctors and patients, but especially in an emergency room. My time at PRH caused me to investigate more into other fields of medicine, which I may not have explored without first volunteering there.
Intern at Subcentro de Salud Ficoa de Montalvo
Summer 2011
I was selected to intern at the Subcentro de Salud FIcoa de Montalvo because of my pre-med and Spanish classes as part of my study abroad experience. The clinic is located in a poor neighborhood in Guayaquil, Ecuador. I was involved both in and out of the clinic four days a week for four hours each day. In the clinic, I primarily took and recorded height, weight and blood pressure of the patients. Outside of the clinic, I would go to local schools and do the same activities and conduct vision tests, give vaccinations, and ask the children about their home life and body development. *Hearts in Motion*
Spring Break 2012
I went to Guatemala for ten days during Spring break of 2012. I was selected to participate due to my Spanish speaking abilities and pre-med classes. I was directly involved with helping the patients and conducting medical consultations, which included translating for about 150 different patients for the doctors and PAs that did not speak Spanish. I translated directions for medications to patients in the recovery room. We worked to help improve the lives of the people while we are there by repairing cleft lips/palates, going to various locations in Guatemala to provide medicine and health services to under-served people, and to help build an addition to the Hearts in Motion eyeglass clinic.

Most Meaningful

My short time with Hearts in Motion (HIM) was the accumulation of my four years of Spanish studying. On the first day of the trip I was selected to go to the hospital to translate for the surgeons. What I thought would be a good day actually turned out to be one of the most difficult. Every year, HIM sends maxillofacial surgeons to Zacapa to fix cleft lips/palates, but this year there was a deadly flu passing through the region. I had to turn people away until next year, which was extremely difficult to do until I realized that we were protecting them since an infection could kill the patient. The following three days I was selected to work hand-in-hand with a doctor or a physician's assistant in a traveling clinic. As the patients told the doctor their problem, I was the person that translated the dialog so that each person understood what the other wanted to say. While I had some trouble with dialog at first, by the end of the trip I was able to anticipate what questions the doctors would ask and have the answer ready for them. I was able to use all the Spanish that I had learned and translated for over 150 patients. The gratification of giving the patient something as simple as vitamins was rewarding. The doctors admitted that the trip would not have been possible without the student translators.

Personal Statement


This personal statement is my own and is meant to show you what a personal statement might look like. While you may read it, please refrain from using any part of it in your own personal statement. It is exactly like it sounds like, personal. These events all touched my life in a way that I felt were important enough to put down on my medical school application and I hope you have unique events too that you could use. Do not copy and paste any part of the application because medical schools can use Google and have the essay traced right back to here. If they find you plagiarized your personal statement expect to have your application thrown away.

Click to read personal statement.

She looked up to me, smiling. We could not have looked more different. She being an Ecuadorian schoolgirl, so small from lack of food and yet pure in her white school dress and wearing her new glasses while I stood beside her, a stark contrast; tall, white, blonde hair and blue eyes. The rest of the school persisted in a calm chaos. She did not have much to say, only one word, "Gracias." I did not need to hear the word; her face had already told me before she had the chance. I could not spend more time with her because I had to continue to test the vision of the children at her school. If I had any regret, it was that I never learned her name, but I will never forget her face.

My original goal was to be a doctor and I immediately decided to major in Zoology at Washington State University. I enrolled in mostly science classes, but I decided to take a Spanish course as I was registering. I intended to take a few courses, just enough to understand basic words patients said. However, four years later, I am graduating with a B.S. in Zoology and a B.A. in Spanish from WSU. My choice to get a B.A. in Spanish did not happen until recently, following a series of events that took me from North to South America, meeting people like the girl above and many others along the way. These experiences have solidified my original decision to become a doctor.

During my spring sophomore semester, I began to volunteer in the Pullman Regional Hospital, and I found myself attracted to the ER. There, I went from room to room to record vitals of the patients and talk to them, conferring with doctors about treatments and medicines. A single patient, however, influenced me more than any patient before. An immigrant came and after attempts at contacting an interpreter failed, I hesitantly stated that I spoke Spanish though I had never spoken Spanish outside the classroom. The patient had concerns about a wound and the interpreter called after I translated the initial worries. While I saw the demand of Spanish speaking doctors, through a self-critical analysis, I discovered that I lacked many words in my Spanish vocabulary to communicate effectively. My initial goal of being able to understand a few words was not sufficient and I knew I had a weakness that I could correct. My motivation and ambition to speak Spanish lead me to study abroad in Ecuador during the summer of 2011 to participate in a medical Spanish program.

My time in Ecuador was busy since I had two jobs; I was both a student at university and a volunteer at a clinic in one of the poorest neighborhoods in Guayaquil, Ecuador. Every day had a schedule that required versatility; I went to my internship for four hours, had classes for another four and a few hours of commuting in between. My enthusiasm for helping people like the little Ecuadorian schoolgirl encouraged me to continue and thrive. My main jobs in the clinic were to go to local schools to give immunizations, take vitals and conduct general health screenings and vision tests of the students. After a month, I began to speak to patients more openly. By the second month I was proficient in Spanish. The doctors allowed me to help teach the children about brushing their teeth and eating a balanced diet. Rather than treat symptoms, we taught basic preventative medicine to fix the problem. The little changes and help we provided can forever change lives, and I am eager to be a part of that. Soon after returning from Ecuador, I saw the short-term benefits of becoming a doctor.

The medical mission trip called Hearts in Motion was a ten day trip to Guatemala in which doctors go abroad to repair cleft lip and palate. I was selected to participate because of my passion for medicine and the ability to speak Spanish. My favorite part was the health clinic where I was responsible for translating the dialog between the physician and the patient. During my short stay, I translated for over 150 patients. I recall one patient vividly. A grandmother approached me in the dental clinic with black stubs as teeth. She could barely speak because of the pain, but she asked if we could remove all of her teeth. Worried about not understanding correctly, I decided to seek a second opinion on the translation. I went to my professor and asked her to double check, which she verified I correctly translated. As each tooth was extracted, I held the grandmother's hand, reassuring her that everything was going well. After all fourteen teeth were removed, the grandmother smiled with her stitched gums and said the same one word the small, Ecuadorian girl said, "Gracias." Being able to speak a patient's maternal language has a soothing effect, and I desire to bring this to medicine. After translating during the trip, I shocked myself with how I could communicate effectively with patients.

My background has allowed me to see the healthcare setting from many different aspects. With my passion to help, self-motivation to learn and tenacity, I believe I have a lot to offer the medical profession. My unique quality, however, is my ambition and experiences in both North and South America, which showed me how much I can do. My most rewarding and meaningful experiences have involved serving others, and I hope to continue this work as a physician.



MCAT Information


Take the MCAT seriously. I didn't the first time I took it. I only planned three weeks of study time and then took the test. I left the day after my MCAT to study abroad. This was a huge mistake and one thing that I regretted ever doing.

The second time I took the MCAT, I planned out a solid two month study plan. Again, this may be short, but I knew I could study hard and get higher than my first MCAT of a 28. The first month was intense review. I read and reread the Princeton Review MCAT book multiple times. I did practice problems from EK and practiced verbal by reading magazines and practice passages from EK 101.

During the second month, I did practice tests and focused on my weak areas. About two weeks before the test, I was burned out and quit studying. I did a few practice problems and just casually read the book. I was consistently scoring above a 30 with 10s in all sections so I quit trying to raise the score and just focused on maintaining it. I wish I hadn't of done this because I could have scored in the high 30s on the real thing and gotten into medical schools much easier. Alas, I didn't and there is nothing I can do to change it. Please don't make this mistake.

Looking back at the process, I would do some things differently. I didn't research each school enough. I applied to Howard, a historically URM school, despite being a white male. I didn't know that when I first applied, that's why I never submitted the secondary. I also applied to NEOMED, which is mostly a BS/MD school. Again, I didn't know this. I applied because of the mission statement and their average MCAT score was lower. It was going to be a safety school, but I didn't have a chance there because a lot of their class is made up of BS/MD students.

What did I think I did well? Personally, I like some of my personal statement. It could have flowed better, but I feel like having a repetitive word that is foreign (Gracias) linked the beginning and the end together. It caused the reader to remember it. I also made the end a big strong conclusion, kind of like a call to action to give me an interview. Looking back I would make that more of a middle of the essay and the focal point.

I also tried to make my 15 experiences tell more of a story for me. I feel like I did that well. I really do have a desire to work with the underserved hispanic community and I wanted to make that a point of my application. I think it worked since I got 8 interviews, but I would only do this if you really wanted to work with the underserved like this.
