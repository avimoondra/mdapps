Personal Characteristics/Diversity Essay

Prompt: Please describe any personal characteristics and/or important or challenging experiences you have had that will contribute to the diversity (broadly defined) of or provide educational benefits to the student body.

I have had the valuable experience of growing up in different cultures. I was born in India, moved with my family to Canada when I was twelve and then to the United States at the age of fourteen. Each time we moved, I was exposed to new ideas and different people, bolstering my love for learning. I enjoyed discovering the nuances of the new accents, trying strange foods, and assimilating to a new culture, each very different from the last. After the move from India to Canada, I had access to the Internet for the first time. My fascination with this �new� technology inspired me to learn more about it. Over the following months, I taught myself web-programming languages and started a web designing business. My desire to teach is an outgrowth of my love for learning. In college, I trained fellow students in web development and hired them on a part-time basis to complete projects for my company. Through my teaching experiences as an undergraduate peer-tutor in Physics and Biology, I discovered that not everyone learns in the same way. I became aware of the importance of flexibility and simplicity, not only in teaching, but also in my interactions with others.

My exposure to disparate cultures enhanced my ability to relate to people from varying backgrounds. The first time I met the soccer team of four-year-olds I was to coach, I recognized the anxiety in their behavior; they hesitated to make eye contact or leave their parents' side. I remembered a similar feeling when I arrived in what seemed like a new world, having to find common ground with new people. I faced these situations many times, and each time I learned important lessons that helped me grow a little more as an individual. Utilizing the lessons I learned, I enjoyed making connections with my team members and helped them learn to relate better with each other. Assimilating to new situations is always challenging, but it gets easier with practice.

My parents made many sacrifices to reach America for the singular purpose of providing my brother and me with better educational opportunities. Along this journey, I had the opportunity to acquire a diverse cache of experiences and develop a passion for the educational opportunities available to me through the field of medicine. I hope to bring my empathetic nature and enthusiasm for learning to medicine and look forward to sharing with, and learning from the rest of the student body.




Personal Statement 2010 - This is what I applied and got in with (surprisingly)

Prompt: Explain your motivation to seek a career in MEDICINE. Be sure to include the value of your experiences that prepare you to be a physician.

There was a train wreck! I grabbed my trauma kit and scrambled out of the ambulance with the rest of my emergency medical technician crew. One of the three damaged passenger cars had fallen on its side, its entrance severely constricted. Our team was divided into groups to cover each of the cars, with my team assigned to the car on its side. I felt a surge of adrenaline as we cautiously made our way into the compartment, laden with a bulky trauma kit. A pregnant woman�s cries rose above the rest.

This was just a simulation. The family and friends of the EMT students were the patients, dressed up with plastic molds, fake blood and makeup to simulate the appearance of injuries. The train wreck was among the many emergency simulations at the TEEX Emergency Services Training Institute. As I applied the knowledge I acquired and skills I learned over the last five months, I experienced how overwhelming it can be to turn practice into reality. Despite the stress induced by the patients� constant shouting for help amidst the initial confusion, I maintained my composure. I approached my first patient, a woman with a broken arm sustained in a fall. After introducing myself and reassuring her, I assessed her vitals and stabilized her arm with a splint. As we loaded her onto a backboard and carried her to the ambulance, I felt fulfilled knowing that I helped a person, even though the whole thing was merely a dramatization.

But then, the simulation suddenly became real when my partner, Chris, slammed his head against a sharp edge while exiting the train. He fell to the ground, unconscious. He was bleeding from a cut on his head, and one of his teeth was fractured. I called another team member for help, and then applied dressing to the cut to stop the bleeding. While another student stabilized the spine, I applied a cervical collar. Just as I finished obtaining vitals, the instructor and other EMTs arrived and we loaded my partner into the ambulance and took him to the hospital. Chris suffered only a minor injury, and he recovered almost immediately. Although I was nervous at the onset of the simulation and had some concerns about my skill level and ability to apply my classroom training to real-life performance, this experience proved to me that I had the ability to perform under pressure, take charge when necessary, and work effectively on a team in a stressful situation.

Once I started working in the emergency room, I provided better care with each patient contact. Hesitant at first to talk to patients, I chose to observe how the nurses and doctors handled them. I eventually developed my communication skills and soon was permitted to manage patients with little supervision. The amazingly rewarding experience of helping patients on emergency sites and in the emergency room reinforced my goal of pursuing a career in medicine.

I developed an ability to empathize with others and communicate effectively not only from my volunteer work as an EMT, but also from the extensive tutoring I did throughout my undergraduate years. In addition to volunteering at the university�s peer-tutoring service, I served a didactic role in study groups for nearly every class I took. When I participated in a study group for biochemistry, an especially challenging class, my peers asked me to conduct a review session before each exam. My old study habits surfaced, and I spent several hours poring over notes, textbooks, and websites gaining a good understanding of the underlying concepts of the material in order to effectively explain the ideas to the group. This led to making my own pre-exam lectures which I presented to a study group of about twenty students. I was rewarded for my efforts not only by gaining a better understanding of the material, but also earning better grades and experiencing the satisfaction that I helped my peers.

My fascination with computers and technology began in elementary school, and by my freshman year in high school I started a web designing business, Faber Designs, that I continue to maintain on a part-time basis. Working as a web designer taught me important lessons in communicating effectively with clients and managing deadlines. In college, I combined my interest in computer science with scientific research in a neuroscience lab where I worked on the structural characterization of intraluminal synaptic vesicle filaments. In addition to performing traditional lab work, I wrote code for software that uses electron microscope images of synaptic vesicles at various angles to create three-dimensional reconstructions of the vesicles.

The discipline I learned from balancing time between academics, volunteering, and my web designing business has been crucial to achieving my goals. After working as an EMT, I can honestly say that helping others fulfills me unlike anything I ever experienced. I am certain that a career in medicine will allow me to use my academic ability and dedication to service to achieve that sense of fulfillment.

Personal Statement 2011 - revamped for reapplication; ended up not needing to reapply

Prompt: Explain your motivation to seek a career in MEDICINE. Be sure to include the value of your experiences that prepare you to be a physician.

There was a train wreck! I grabbed my trauma kit and scrambled out of the ambulance with the rest of my EMT crew. One of the three damaged passenger cars had fallen on its side, its entrance severely constricted. I felt a surge of adrenaline as we cautiously made our way into a train compartment, laden with a bulky trauma kit. It was only a simulation, staged near the end of my EMT training, but suddenly it became all too real.

My partner, Chris, hit his head on a sharp edge while exiting the train. He fell to the ground, unconscious and bleeding. Although on the verge of becoming overwhelmed, I maintained my composure, called another team member for help, and applied a dressing to stop the bleeding. Just as I finished obtaining vitals, the instructor and other EMTs arrived, and we loaded my partner into the ambulance. This experience left me with an appreciation for the ability to employ knowledge and training in stressful situations.

My understanding and appreciation of applied knowledge increased when I volunteered in the Emergency Room and shadowed doctors. I marveled at physicians as they collected patient information, synthesized the data to formulate a diagnosis, and explained their findings to the patients. They were driven by a passion for science and a desire to serve as they implemented their excellent interpersonal skills. My experiences confirm my belief that I will make an effective physician, as I share the same passions.

Along with my coursework in Biology, research has been one of my most satisfying scientific pursuits. I am able to use my love for science and develop my intellectual curiosity by applying acquired knowledge to solve problems. I assisted Dr. Harlow, setting up his lab and isolating synaptic vesicles. Once the bench work began, I experienced frustrations: experiments stalled while waiting for equipment to arrive, failed experiments, contaminated samples, and long hours. But I thrived on the dedication required and hard work demanded by the research, experiences that will undoubtedly prepare me for the pressures of medical school. I am currently working on a presentation of my work in developing an optimized protocol for isolating synaptic vesicles, a manuscript which will be submitted for publication early this fall.

In addition to academics, I remain dedicated to service. My experience volunteering as an Emergency Room Technician confirmed my desire to be a doctor. As the first hospital contact for walk-in patients, I am responsible for obtaining patients' relevant medical history, symptoms, and vital signs. After observing the nurses and conducting patient interviews myself, I developed an ability to ask relevant questions based on information related by frequently anxious patients and was able to put them at ease in uncomfortable situations. From my ER experiences, I discovered the importance and benefits of the doctor-patient relationship and welcome the opportunity to interact personally with patients. Often, I find myself listening to patients' concerns and stories and helping to allay their fears. I take comfort in the knowledge that I am assisting in make a patient�s hospital stay more pleasant, an experience that is more rewarding than I ever imagined.

Beyond research and volunteering, I worked on my interpersonal skills, an important component in the practice of medicine. I recall a valuable lesson I learned from Benjamin, a four-year-old I coached on my U5 soccer team, who informed me that he would play only if I gave him candy. Shortly after promising him a reward, he scored a goal�for the other team. He came to me to collect his treat, claiming that he had, in fact, �scored a goal.� In that moment I learned the importance of unambiguous communication. As the owner of a web designing business that I started in high school, I learned to communicate with clients, manage deadlines, and build lasting business relationships. In my sophomore year of college, I hired other students on a part-time basis to complete web-designing projects. Coordinating and managing the business has been a tremendous help in developing my communication and leadership skills.

There is no single event that ignited my desire to become a doctor, but rather a lifetime of experiences. My continued commitment to service, research, and clinical activities have galvanized my decision to pursue a career in medicine. My love for science and my ability to communicate effectively will serve me well. I am certain that as a doctor, I will be intellectually challenged and content knowing that I am using my passions and skills to help others.
