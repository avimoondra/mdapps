Interview #1
The University of Michigan
September 8, 2006

Reflections Pro
The admissions staff shows how they care about students. The community seems welcoming, relaxed, no competition. The use of technology is amazing: the 11.1 GB microscopy demo to the step-by-step patient videos to the quizzes, etc. The students seem down to earth and make time for fun on weekends. The school gives a 10 week summer break and seems to actually care about students e.g. 2 yr deferrals. The Family Centered Experience is extraordinary. This program enables students for 2 yrs to follow the same patient that has a chronic illness to understand context, sociology, and other issues outside of pure pathology. This seems to reflect the school?s desire to train compassionate students. The school also has decently high board scores (avg ~2 stdev above average). Ann Arbor (120,000 people) is nice, the town is green.

Reflections Con
The 170 person class size is abnormally large. This might lead to anonymity, which is also how the 39,000+ students would (might?) make me feel. Coming from Rice, this is a drastic change in community setting. The school seems accepting of Asians (overall school ~25% Asian), but I feel like it?s WASPed out otherwise. This is perhaps due to the high number of Michigan residents since it?s a public school. The school just seemed white, dunno. Rankings wise, U of M is above average but perhaps not spectacular. The students have fun, but there?s the issue too much fun? And then there?s the weather. Snow and lots of it. Some of the facilities seemed old, but I think the school is transitioning--some really old stuff and some new stuff going in (cardiovascular, genetics, children?s/women?s, etc. LOTS of new construction). The tuition is also a huge hurdle, but that?s the name of the game.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Interview #2
Baylor Medical School
September 22, 2006

Reflections Pro
Baylor is cheap! Baylor does a phenomenal job of keeping the cost down for Texans and out of staters alike. Baylor is part of the largest medical complex in the world! TMC has diversity of clinical experiences, which was listed by both interviewers as important strength. Baylor is ranked highly and has high avg board scores. The curriculum is really, really flexible. Baylor students have a lot of free time (emphasis added). Very laid back, non-competitive.

Reflections Con
Baylor is in Texas right next to Rice! I know (and will know) a ton of people going there. Perhaps too flexible (?) or unguided, I can?t really tell on that. The biggest, biggest con is that it?s in Houston and across the street from my undergrad. For me, it is limited personal growth in that regard.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Interview #3
Harvard Medical School
October 23, 2006

Harvard Pro
The people and resources at Harvard are outstanding. The amount of research is unparalleled. With the edition of the New Research Building (NRB), you can see how Harvard produces quality research. The opportunities are there. Boston is a very nice city, with a NE feel while staying small and green. The history and parks in Boston make it a great place to live (in addition to the public transport system). I like how most 1st and 2nd years live in Vanderbuilt Hall- a dorm with decent facilities. The feel I get is that if you want to do something with your life, the doors are openable but perhaps not inviting. Meaning, if you?re proactive you can do anything, but people don?t guide you through or hold your hand. The teaching facilities I got just a glimpse of. For example, I got to practice on one of the patient-mannequins. I likewise feel that I got only a small glimpse of Harvard?s greatness overall?For example, on the interview day, the admissions department neglected to mention that they?re ranked #1 (research) by US News and World Reports. Perhaps they just forgot.

Harvard Con
The first thing that stands out in my mind is the issues with the New Curriculum. The New Pathway was developed in the 80s. Twenty years later, Harvard decided to finally change how it structures the program. They start with the Biochem/Development block, cut the first year down by a month or so, chop off a little bit of breaks, and basically just squeeze years one and two down by a month or two. Then, they add ethics/others as mandatory and put in a longitudinal program. This seems like it stresses years one and two out. There have been complaints by the class of 2010 because of the lack of free time in the New New Pathway. Will they change it for my graduating class? Perhaps, but perhaps not.

It?s very hard because many of my questions weren?t addressed in full length. For example, the residency matching placement performance is a bit mysterious. Harvard should do exceedingly well, one would hope, but the ability of the school to equip its students and facilitate their growth is still in question in my mind. It?s hard because Harvard makes no attempt at selling itself. Is Harvard good? Yes. Is Harvard great? Probably. Is Harvard amazing (as a place for medical education)? Maybe. In my case, Harvard?s silence is either one of two cases: either their lack of salesmanship is a humble, actions-speak-for-themselves attitude, or their silence is the arrogant, stereotypically NE self-righteousness that I would like to avoid.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Interview #4
Washington University in St. Louis
October 27, 2006

Wash U Pro
The research at Wash U is phenomenal. Clearly one of the top 5 research med schools in the country, Wash U is a premier research institution. Also, it didn?t seem like the students lacked clinical ability. Going in, I was afraid Wash U would be all research, but they have significant clinical exposure. There are plenty of opportunities for research. I feel like if I wanted to do ANY research I could. There?s even a 6 week program for research the summer before you start at Wash U (paid). The facilities in Wash U all connect so you can stay dry. There?s also a new Farrell Education Center, which is the nicest med school building I?ve seen thus far (it was built within the last two years) where most of the classes/class related stuff is held. The Wash U students are obviously amazing (highest avg MCAT) and tuition is fixed (no changes in 4 years). It also seems like you can chill with non-MDs in Olin Hall, which might be nice. St. Louis is pretty cheap and decent. Midwest feel, Barnes-Jewish is a good hospital, and for outdoors stuff Forest Park is boarding campus.

Wash U Con
The focus on research comes at the expense of clinical superiority. I had been advised to watch out for schools that are ranked highly in research but not in primary care; Wash U is such a school. It?s top-5 research rank should be compared with its primary care non-ranking! Is it a good research school? Sure. But for residency you gotta consider primary-care residency director rankings, etc. I?m also not sure St. Louis weather would be?fun. Yeah, so they got some cold, dreary climates going on. I guess Portland-like, but that?s still something to consider. The fact that all facilities link is cool, except that it indicates the fact that weather might suck. The lack of a public health program at Wash U coupled with the lack of anything longitudinal indicates Wash U is trapped in the research bubble?treating diseases, but perhaps failing to see the bigger picture.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Interview #5
Oregon Health & Science University
November 2, 2006

OHSU Pro
The first things that come to mind are location, cost, and primary care. On location: Portland is amazing. If you?re reading this and haven?t been to Portland, you don?t realize what you?re missing. Within two hour drive from OHSU campus you can get to a river, lake, biggest city park in the US, ski resort on Mt. Hood, forest, desert, beach, winery, farm, or city center (top 40 biggest cities in the US). If you like the outdoors, Portland is the place for you. If you like to shop, Portland is for you (no sales tax). People ask: Doesn?t it always rain there? I don?t think you realize it?s a friendly rain. I never ever use an umbrella in Portland. I walked 15 minutes in my suit through the rain, got to the building, paper-toweled off, and was completely dry. Portland is relatively inexpensive tuition-wise for both in- and out-of-staters. On primary care: This school is outright amazing in terms of primary care. If you want to go into family practice, internal medicine, rural medicine, or other similar fields, then please apply here. The attention to this kind of care is outrageously good, ranked 3rd in the nation, and has that kind of tone throughout. For example, there are mandatory rural medicine clerkships where you go into super-small towns and experience that setting. There?s also a pretty cool curriculum where you only take one class at a time (not systems-based). Did I mention that Portland is the microbrew capital of the world? Oh, and they?re also building a new biotech building for research!

OHSU Con
This school is plagued by the Oregon-public-school problems: low quality, low standards, Oregon-focused. Great, if you want to stay in Oregon and do family practice, this place is nice. But, if you want to specialize/be competitive/do ANYTHING else, you?re at a disadvantage. In their residency placement for 2006, I didn?t see any Derm and very few Radiology/ Anesthesiology/ Surgery. Really, they are not competitive for residency. This is seen in their step one averages: a little above the national? That?s terrible in comparison with what these other schools are doing. In perspective, though, their matriculation MCAT is barely above average, so that might make sense. Another big issue, for me, is that they heavily stress/love non-traditional matriculates. OHSU?s average age at matriculation is the highest I?ve ever seen at like 26.5? That severely changes class dynamics; added to that is the lack of on campus housing (decreasing unity) and no affiliated undergrad institution. Their research rank will soon go up, but their reputation is still sad.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Interview #6
Columbia University College of Physicians & Surgeons
November 10, 2006

Columbia Pro
New York. Bam. Your rent at Bard is like 600 a month to stay on Manhatten Island. Damn. The opportunities with living in New York are second to none. The students really are what everybody says they are: really cool. They become super-tight because 95% live in Bard their first year and then in other years they live in the towers. Yeah, honestly, location and student body are hard to beat. It doesn?t really make sense if you haven?t visitied there, but Columbia?s location is sweet. I had this surreal moment when I was walking towards Central Park down Broadway when I was like, ?Oh, wait, this is the actual, famous Broadway.? The class is non-competitive and seems to have one of the best balances of work-play I?ve seen. No need for a car, decent amount of international opportunities, long summer between first-second yrs, you do care rotations by end of third year, all 4th year is elective, oh, and NY-Pres is ranked 6th in the nation.

Columbia Con
Very high percentage of NE ivyness. Curriculum is quite traditional (mostly lecture). No longitudinal component. They wait-list an ass-load of people. Price is very high. Didn?t get the feel that research was that much emphasized/opportunity. Didn?t get the feel that step scores and residency-placement were that amazing?Huge time devoted to anatomy (prob most hard-core anatomy program i.e. good for future surgeons).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Interview #7
University of Pennsylvania
November 17, 2006

Penn Plus
The Penn curriculum?the all-together school component?is the best I?ve seen. Ranked #3 in schools (research), #16 in schools (primary care), #1 in board scores (told to me at Harvard and Columbia), #2 in NIH funding received, #1 oldest med school, #1 oldest hospital, and top-ten hospitals overall, Penn is worth seriously considering. My first fear, going in, was that Penn would be a bunch of arrogant, self-absorbed people, mostly because friends of mine have said so for the past four years (declining the offers) and I?ve seen it on StudentDoctor, but that?s not the impression I got at all. For example, of all those ?# blank? ranking numbers I cited, the only one they mentioned on interview day was the oldest hospital. It seems very, very well-rounded. They really care about community service and global health. Many students go abroad, there?s even a global health ?certificate? thing. Three afternoons off per week. Pass Fail first semester. Philadelphia is a decent city. A huge, huge selling factor, though, is the Curriculum 2000. Developed in 1997-1999, I felt like this curriculum overhaul was actually real. They finish basic science in 1.5 years and are in clinic 1.5 years before taking the boards AFTER third year. That?s why they have high board average. They finish cores before taking the boards. Fourth year is completely elective. They also have a LEAPP (longitudinal) program for 3 years, but it?s a bit disorganized. The faculty seem like they actually listen to students about curriculum issues. I?d like to conclude by saying it is the best, well-rounded curriculum I have seen thus far.

Penn Minus
Community. I feel like they have adopted the commuter-style structure. Nobody lives on campus per se; rather, students live in a disunionized, scattered set of apartments wherever they can find them. My host?s advice was that medical school isn?t like college?it?s really hard to meet people. I?m sorry, but it doesn?t have to be that way. Look at Vandy, Bard, wherever. Granted, the class gets close, but where?s the social connectivity? Similarly, they lack exposure to undergrads, nursing, OT, PT, law, dental?even though they?re on the same campus! Sure, the university atmosphere is better (being med with whole undergrad/campus/etc.) but not if you don?t take advantage of it somehow...House them together, closer classrooms, something...I had been told everybody was NE/Ivy but that?s not the impression I got. Their school seemed a bit white-washed. Because it?s old, the school facilities were merely decent. I guess for me I felt like the education would be amazing, but social connectedness and spiritual growth might be hampered. They?re pretty dang strong for residency placing. It will be a hard decision.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Interview #8
Duke Medical School
February 23, 2007

Duke Pro

The most unique feature of DukeMed is the curriculum. Structured as 12 months basic science, 13 months clinical, one year scholarly pursuit, and one year advanced clinical, the Duke curriculum is very different than any other school in the country. Notably, they?ve had this curriculum for 40 years?not a new tradition or change. The philosophy behind this was interesting: in a changing world, a school cannot teach you everything you need to know, so Duke tries to teach you how to learn. The obvious desire for superficial proof through board scores and placement matching provides evidence contrary to skeptical belief?Duke ties for second-highest board scores but in the third year time. Students in this period have the option of research, tracks, or even a dual-degree. With 30% +/- choosing the second degree (MPH, MBA, etc.), it shows that Duke is not necessarily 100% focused on research. An additional argument for the curriculum is that your first year is 12 months, so your ?one? year is only about 3/4 the length of two eight-month ?years.?

Though Durham is not an amazing town, North Carolina is scenic and enjoyable. An example is the park on Duke Campus. The UNC (Chapel Hill-Raleigh-Durham triangle) probably allows socializing past just the Duke campus. DukeMed does have the advantage of being associated with the undergraduate campus?a benefit I?m just now realizing?that shows itself worthy in the realm of basketball tickets. You probably need a car, but the cost of living is cheap. Let me conclude by re-emphasizing the importance of examining the curriculum closely, for there are many advantages.
