Summary of Application Experience

In a word, indescribable. In all seriousness, I had an awful experience. It was more or less a constantly stressful period that sucked a lot of fun out of my senior year. But! This does not mean you will.

I didn't get my AMCAS in until late Aug. or early Sept. Bad idea. Very bad idea. People will tell you not to procrastinate, and just get the application in, even with only one or two rewrites. These people are Right, capital R. Especially because I ended up applying to 19 MD-PhD programs (your experience may vary if MD only). I know people who applied to 20, 23, 25! (An aside: those who tell you to apply to 10 or 12 programs are wrong. Do Not Listen. I suppose if you are an obscenely overqualified candidate you could apply to fewer, which I am not, but even then, you may turn up hidden gems. UPenn was honestly not in my top third of schools when I applied, and I only belatedly realized it's probably the best place for me of all the schools out there. Had I applied to fewer, I might not have gone for it at all.) Anyways, I had to complete about two secondaries a week in order to get all my apps in on the deadline. I had a bad experience, trying to complete senior year, two majors, a thesis, maintain a relationship, apply, interview, and think. Comparatively, my friend had an easier time as a result of being decisive and effective in getting applications done. The essays are all going to be the same but not enough to recycle, I know. Your responses will start to smudge together in your mind, and you will start to produce mush, I know. Attack them with will and get the applications out the door. Most likely, unless you are a glowing, visionary writer, the strength of your application is already there on paper. Get. It. Out. The. Door.

Interviewing is stressful, but trust me when I say you'll be fine. You know yourself better than anyone, and more likely than not, you are qualified and ready. Spend some time before the whole interview season thinking about the various activities you did and what you got out of them. Trace your progression through life and see what has changed you and how. Sounds trite; it is. But, it will give you things to talk about and relate to. Be interesting. Funny or informative or deeply emotional experiences give you color and background. I had none of the above. But what I did have was the genuine love of pursuing academics and turning them to the service of others. It's easy to toss out prepackaged, respectable sounding answers to trite questions (I mean, they started it), but it's worth thinking about the obvious questions beforehand so they are YOUR tired, retread answers. It may not feel like it, but the interviews are mostly about you. Also remember to think about the program and the years to come. You will get very sick of hearing people ask, 'Do you have any questions?' Always have one. Even if you know the answer. From your previous interviewer.

I had a grueling interview schedule, 11 in the space of 2-3 months. I had one or two a week for several weeks straight. Bring work with you if you think you can get anything done. Most likely, you won't. Interviews are full-day affairs, often including and stretching beyond dinner, and you'll come back to your room exhausted and/or tipsy after potentially 5 or more interviews (Record: WashU in St. Louis with 7 in one day) and a trip to a bar. (MD interviews are generally only 2 interviews, perhaps 3 with a student interview. From what I've read, it's kinda like the life of a soldier--long periods of boredom punctuated by a few really exciting-in-a-bad-way moments.)

Planning trips will be hard, especially considering the little amount of notice you'll often have, no more than a month or two, depending on when you get your applications in and how the process works at individual schools. Get familiar with Amtrak, it's great for shorter trips on the East coast- approximately as expensive, significantly more comfortable and easier than flying. You will most likely find yourself in and out of your residence often, so it becomes second nature to care for your suit, keep a toiletries/medicines bag packed, and be aware of transportation services. Packing is very easy, though. You need to bring only a suit and whatever else is necessary to look professional, a nice-looking portfolio/padfolio/folder, and perhaps an additional change of clothing. So relax, pack light, and enjoy the hospitality the schools offer as far as possible. Barring any enormous blunders (screaming match with an interviewer? Insulting the program or your own application within earshot of, well, anyone? Bad ideas), you will come out the other side mostly pristine and happy.

Expect rejections. They suck. They hurt. It feels like betrayal, like failure, like all your effort was ultimately worth nothing. It was not. The feeling will pass, concentrate on what you still have left. Don't get too attached to a school (until you are accepted), don't think about it while you wait for action on your app, and don't punch hard objects if the outcome isn't what you wanted. The application and selection process is irreducibly random and unavoidably cruel, but generally people end up where they honestly deserve to be.

To all the applicants who read this, I wish you the best of luck. And to matriculating students, OMIGOD WE'REGOINGTOBEDOCTORS /rolleyes. But in all seriousness, my heartfelt congratulations. It was a long way, but now we're finally getting to the good stuff. But remember: always wear comfortable shoes. (Apologies to Caucasian female, 23)

I am currently an entering first year in the MSTP at the University of Pennsylvania. I would be very happy to answer questions or help in any way I can, though my experience is clearly limited to my own.
