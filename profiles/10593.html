Summary of Application Experience

I got a call about acceptance to NJMS early. I really liked the school and its early clinical exposure and very friendly, cooperative students and faculty. Good vibes!

Emory is simply amazing. The campus is beautiful, the students are incredibly nice. Atlanta is a great city to live in. Clinical experience is impressive too. It probably gave me the best 'gut' feeling.

NYU- Wonderful clinical experience, great research, and in the greatest city in the world. It'd be really sweet to live there.

Update: So far it's between Emory, NYU, NJMS, and RWJ
And I got a scholarship to NJ Med!
Wow I feel really blessed.

I'm still deciding.... I think I'll have to wait for 2nd look weekend and financial info to figure out where I'll be happiest and have the best opportunities.

Well I hope this helps anyone considering NYU in the future:
So I went to their revisit weekend- it was incredible! First off, the students are very laid back, which I think is a consequence of the P/F curriculum. They have a test every three weeks, and for the first week after the test they have lots of free time, the week after they're doing a couple hours of work a day, and the week after that they study like crazy. Then, after the test, NYU rents out a bar and the students go nuts. The workload seemed like a lot, but it's doable. One person said that if she wanted to go out every night, she could and not fail. Also, the students are involved in a ridiculous number of activities outside of class. They had about 60 different clubs, which is the most of any medical school. Some of them were for fun (the volleyball club) and some are related to their interests in medicine (the free clinic). They also get funding for pursuing research projects through the school pretty easily (the honors program) and funding to go on conferences around the country. A lot of students even went abroad to help people in underserved areas internationally.
The second two years impressed me even more than the first two. 80% of rotations are done at Bellevue, which takes all of the city's uninsured and immigrant populations. Over 87 different languages are spoken there each year, and students really see everything there is to see. They're also given a ridiculous amount of autonomy and responsibility during the rotations. They draw blood, put in IVs, make incisions, do sutures. One guy even did an entire operation by himself (with the supervision of an attending)! A lot of places would never allow you to do any of that stuff (particularly if your rotations are done at a private hospital). So it seems med students are really prepared to go into their residency because they've done most of the residency-related stuff before.
Were there any disadvantages? Well, the housing isn't the nicest. Most students live in dorm style setups the first two years, which is good for bonding with your fellow students but bad for living a good lifestyle I guess. Personally, I could see myself doing it. After all, we have the rest of our lives to live in nice houses or apartments. Also, I hear the financial aid is really bad, so students there have to be prepared to be in debt (for me it's prob going to be 200k). Yes, the average debt figures reported by MSAR are very low, but this is probably due to the amount of students who have wealthy families.
By now you can tell that I was really impressed. I know I'd be happy there and confident in my education, but will I go there next year? I have to wait to revisit NJMS and Emory, and get financial packages from everybody. But to those definitely going to NYU- you have a lot to look forward to!

NJMS:
Well, I went back to NJMS and got to talk with a bunch of students, the head of the humanism center, and the head of the Physician's Core curriculum. Lots of people took time out of their busy schedules just to talk to me, and it was great! Everyone was incredibly nice. Students at NJMS are busy every day between 9 and 5, attending class, lab, small group discussions, and PBL usually. One day each week they visit a physician's office and shadowing, but I wasn't sure if students liked this or felt that they didn't have enough time for it. The biggest thing that they stress about NJMS is clinical experience. Students get to do a lot of the same things that NYU students did. This is the advantage of doing rotations at a big public hospital. I also checked out their match list, and it was surprisingly really good! 8 people alone went to Yale, at least 2 went to Harvard, and I just remember seeing a lot of big names. The students told me it really doesn't matter where you go to med school as long as you perform well, and I believe it.
The disadvantages of the school were to be expected. It's located in Newark, NJ, which is not a very good area. Students need to go in groups if they want to walk around, but the new dorm buildings are connected to the school. Also, obviously, the name of the school is not very good. Of course, everyone would like to go to a big name school, but it seems to not matter in the long run.

Another update:
I've pretty much, very very painfully decided on either NYU or Emory. I can't help but think that I'm about to shoot myself in the foot with all the debt I'm about to incur, likely a quarter million dollars, before ever making a dime! When I started this whole process, I was actually hoping to go to NJMS, and I got my dream plus a full ride. But after a lot of research about the school, the situation is just too unstable for me. However, I'm certain that this is just a short term thing and the school will be able to come out of it. But hey, I'm only going to live once, and I might as well take things one step at a time and go where I'll be happiest without worrying about debt.

Drumroll...
It's NYU! Several factors led this to be my ultimate choice. They gave me a better fin aid package than Emory (all loans and a very very small scholarship, but at least I don't have to search for private loans). And I feel awesome about this decision. If I were to ask myself, which place out of all the schools I applied to would make me the best Doc I can be, the answer is without a doubt NYU (see my above rant). I would love to see Emory again when I'm looking for residencies- I'm in love with the ATL! In terms of loan scariness- as long as I'm able to feed a family and keep a roof over their heads, that's all I need. Anyways, I'm so excited. Good luck to everybody looking at this.
