Summary of Application Experience

I was a bit late in the application process -- the personal statement took me far longer than I expected, and so I only submitted my primary in early/mid September. I took a scattershot approach to which schools I applied to, using primarily the criteria of 1. being a great school I would happily attend, and 2. where I wanted to live for the next 4 years.

Primary app submitted 9/8/2005
Verified 9/16/2005

9/19/2005

Secondaries received from Columbia, Yale, Case Western, Stanford, University of Michigan, Cornell, Baylor, Wash U.

10/12/2005

All secondaries received, including screened, except UCSF, USC, and University of Pittsburgh. Submitted secondaries: Wash U, Stanford, Case Western, NYU, Baylor, Northwestern, UCLA, U Michigan

11/14/2005

23 secondaries submitted. The only ones I passed on were UI Chicago ($52K/out of state tuition??? And a very bureaucratic secondary) and USC (so far -- I may still give in and do it). I'm complete at most places, though there seems to be a snafu with at least U of Pittsburgh and my LORs (my career service sent the wrong LOR to them...). Interviews at UW (completed) and two upcoming at Duke and Wake Forest! Woohoo! It's strange to be sitting here waiting...

12/2/2005

I was accepted to UW! Wow, that was fast... and apparently early in the cycle. Nice to get that monkey off my back, and UW is an awesome school. I'm still going to go to most interviews (if I get them), largely because I want to get as much info as possible before making my decision, and I really don't know all that much about the different schools. But I'm glad I didn't apply to UIC and USC now, as I definitely would not have chosen those places over UW.

Interview invites received so far: UW, Wake Forest, Duke, UMichigan, WashU, Johns Hopkins (today!)

I've been extremely fortunate in this application process so far... (but am still wondering about UCSF! No secondary, but no rejection yet... and I really like the place.)

12/8/2005

Went to WashU, and really liked the students, the school, and the administration. Such down-to-earth, nice people! Definitely at odds with their ridiculous MCAT and GPA averages. Only thing is that I don't think I would enjoy living in St. Louis...

Just received invites from Case Western and Cleveland Clinic Lerner College of Medicine in one e-mail, and Yale. I've only gone on one interview trip, and I'm already tired from travelling... argh. I guess this is a good problem to have.

1/2/2005

Pre-secondary rejection from UCSF. Ouch! Thinking of writing them and appealing the decision... guess it couldn't hurt. Also a post-secondary rejection from UChicago, and a pre-interview hold from UPenn.

In a week, I'm going to leave for my extended interview trip... 7 interviews in 3 weeks, followed by 2 more interviews in the following week. Woohoo! Won't be back until end of January. I'm excited, but I feel rusty on my interviewing skills! I felt so poised and focused at the end of writing all my secondaries -- this process is really long and drawn out.

2/1/2006

Back from my long interview trip. 3 weeks, 8 schools, 4 in the last week. Whee! I really had a great time at lots of the places when I visited. Every place had their pros and cons, but I think I would be really happy at any of these schools: Johns Hopkins, Duke, Columbia, or Yale... I liked UMich a lot, but I'm hesitant about moving to Ann Arbor. Johns Hopkins was extremely professional and had a cohesiveness and history I didn't observe at other places -- very impressive. Everything was well done there, and priority and attention seemed to be focused in the right places (patients, students, science, and community). I loved the administration at Yale -- they put me in touch with a student who is considering cooking school so I could advise him! And the opportunity to do MD/JD... are you kidding me??? At Yale Law?? Freakin' amazing... Columbia offers amazing training and peers to be around, not to mention an amazing location and housing in the city. I had great conversations with some of the students there. I was surprised by how much I liked Duke -- I thought I wouldn't like Durham. My experience was probably colored a lot by my student host -- an amazing guy and his wife who were people I respect and admire, and I met his friends and looked at the school through his perspective. I like the Duke curriculum, because it makes a lot of sense to get to clinicals sooner, and I love the independent year of research/scholarly pursuit.

Now just hoping I get into some of these places...

Next week I'm off to Ohio!

2/8/2006

Just got back from Cleveland Clinic and Case. It was surprisingly impressive -- I loved the emphasis on clinical medicine and international health at Case, and Dean Horwitz is pretty darn enthusiastic about what he wants to achieve there. I also liked the students I met there... Cleveland Clinic was interesting, very business focused, oriented. It was definitely a different feel and community than any of the other medical schools I visited, but during the day and tour we got to see and talk about things in far more depth than any other med school (saw and talked to various labs about the work being done there).

This is going to be interesting...

3/22/06

Whew! Well, since the last update, I've interviewed at UPitt and Baylor, been accepted to 6 schools, and waitlisted at many more! Of the places I've interviewed, I have to say that Baylor has been the darkhorse. It wasn't one of my top choices, but now I know more about Houston and Baylor, it's a really tempting choice. Really cheap tuition, good, diverse population (= interesting patients AND great ethnic restaurants), GREAT cost of living, with the downside being the heat and humidity, and 'suburby' feel of the city.

Case Western and Cleveland Clinic second look weekends coming up... looking forward to meeting some of my fellow prospectives! The people I go to medical school with will be a big factor in my decision, and I'm looking forward to meeting some interesting folks...

6/30/06

It's been quite a while since I've updated. On May 14th, I was deciding between UMich and Yale -- a really tough choice. The revisit weekend for UMich was really awesome; I loved the people I met, and Ann Arbor seemed very pleasantly liveable. They also gave me a really nice scholarship. At the end of the day, though, I decided on Yale -- awesome curriculum, freedom to pursue your interests, and location on the East Coast (where I really wanted to experience), along with a still stellar match list. And their financial aid was kick-ass, especially since I qualified to be an independent student (because of age). The final kicker? Everyone there seemed ridiculously happy.

The day after the deadline, Duke accepted me off their waitlist. But I found that my initial love affair with Duke had cooled off somewhat -- other information and insights that I had received after my interview made me think that perhaps my experience at Duke would not be the halcyon one that I imagined, based on my interview period. The kicker here was that they offered me far less financial aid than Yale, at the tune of $90K less. So, Yale here I come!

Not so fast. In early June, I was accepted off the waitlist to Johns Hopkins. Of all the schools, I was most impressed by Hopkins on my interview day. Professional, well-run, organized, and intensely dedicated summarize my impressions of the school and the people. My sense was that in the atmosphere at Hopkins, I would be pushed to work really hard, and come out superbly trained and very well positioned for whatever I wanted to pursue in medicine. The expectations were higher coming out of Hopkins. 'Best of the best' (motto at Hopkins) seems arrogant, but reflects an expectation and a tradition that demands the best from its students, graduates, house staff, and administration. In contrast, Yale's motto at the revisit weekend was 'Have it your way -- Yale Med.' The people at Yale were amazing -- super talented, diverse, and passionate. They are going to do well in whatever they choose to do, but that focus doesn't need to be medicine. No pressure, plenty of opportunities to explore.

Hopkins and Yale -- opposite ends of the spectrum, when it came to curriculum and educational atmosphere. For each of the schools, I was excited about different things. For Yale, I was excited by the non-medicine opportunities -- interdisciplinary collaborative research, potentially attending the law school, pursuing outside interests, visiting New York. For Hopkins, I was excited about the medical training and the rigorous education, and NOT for Baltimore or being able to pursue outside interests.

It was a really tough choice, but at the end of the day, I chose Hopkins. What made the choice possible, too, was that their financial aid ended up being comparable to Yale's financial aid.

So, it's off to Baltimore for me!
